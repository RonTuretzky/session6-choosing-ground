Two questions, not twenty forms

Forget the paperwork zoo. Holding a commune together comes down to separating two layers — then copying a template that already works.

🏛️ Who holds it

A trust, foundation or community land trust owns the land — permanently, off the market, with no owner to buy out. It can never be sold out from under the community.

🤝 Who runs it

A cooperative operates on top: it farms, trades, employs, and shares the surplus — usually one-member-one-vote. Where foreigners can't own land, a company holds title for the co-op.

🌾 Who's done it

You don't have to invent this. Communities the world over have held and farmed land together for generations — living templates you can adapt.

Lock the land in a trust. Run the farm as a co-op. Copy a template that's lasted a century.

Deliberately high-level per feedback. The one idea: separate the permanent land-HOLDING layer (trust/foundation/CLT — never sold) from the OPERATING layer (co-op that trades and employs). The Mennonite colonies in Paraguay/Bolivia are the strongest proof a foreign group can do this for generations. Vehicle-by-vehicle mechanics belong in a handout, not the slide.

Walk the class through combos: a US-citizen retiree with a foreign pension moving to territorial Panama pays 0% locally but still files to the IRS (FEIE + credits); the same person to worldwide Spain without a regime pays progressive rates; farm income is local-source so it's taxed everywhere, but a co-op is taxed once at the member. The lesson: match your income type to a territorial or special-regime country, and remember a US passport never fully switches off. Regimes are closing (PT/ES/GR 2023–26) — check the year.

🚧 …and then: can you build on it?

Agricultural land ≠ buildable. Italy caps building at 0.03 m³/m² and wants a registered farmer. Greece needs a 4,000 m² minimum + road frontage. Spain's anti-fragmentation rules cap subdivision; Portugal's protected soils forbid building outright.

Cheap land you can't own — or can't build on — scores a zero, whatever the price tag says.

There is no "commune visa"

No country admits an intentional community as a unit. Every adult qualifies individually — a 5–30 person move is 5–30 parallel cases. So you mix routes:

RouteWho it fitsWhere
🏖️ Passive-income / retirementMembers with pensions or savingsItaly ERV · Spain NLV · Portugal D7 · Costa Rica pensionado
💻 Digital nomadRemote earnersGreece · Croatia · Georgia · Sri Lanka (2026)
🚜 Entrepreneur / self-employedThe farm co-op itselfPortugal D2 · Spain autónomo
🚪 Easy-residency / low-barThe whole group at onceParaguay · Panama · Uruguay · Georgia (1-yr visa-free)
🧬 Ancestry / diasporaEligible membersGhana Right of Abode · EU ancestry

Real groups run a patchwork: retirees on passive-income visas, workers on nomad/entrepreneur visas, a few who won't qualify at all. Budget a lawyer and a staggered, multi-year arrival — Paraguay is the rare place you can move the whole group easily.

Four ways people actually do it

🌾 The depopulation play
ItalySpainPortugalGreeceJapan

Cheap emptying villages, high quality of life, pensioner tax breaks. The wall is the one-by-one visa. Best if your group skews toward retirees living on pensions or savings — they're the ones who qualify for the passive-income visas.

🚪 Easy-residency Latin
ParaguayUruguayPanamaEcuador

Move the whole group in with little friction, own land freely, pay no tax on foreign income — and there are communities here that have farmed together for generations. The trade: weaker institutions.

💻 Low-tax digital
GeorgiaSerbiaAlbaniaEstonia

1-year visa-free entry, flat or no-foreign-income tax, dirt cheap. The catch is owning the land (Georgia bars farmland). Best if members keep remote jobs and pool the income.

🛡️ Quality-of-life premium
Costa RicaNew ZealandUruguay

Top-tier safety, healthcare and stability. You just pay more for the land, and New Zealand's visa is hard. Choose this when peace of mind matters more than price.

The Session-5 cost stack — by jurisdiction

The economies of scale from S5 don't travel uniformly. Healthcare and pensions especially swing on where you land. (Each country's own read sits on its globe card.)

🏥 Healthcare

Best: universal & cheap — Spain, Portugal, Italy, Greece, Costa Rica, Uruguay.

Catch: rural gaps everywhere; Georgia & Nepal poor outside cities; US cost.

👵 Pensions

Best: foreign pension untaxed — Georgia, Panama, Paraguay, Uruguay; 7–10% regimes in Italy / Greece / Portugal.

Catch: worldwide-tax home countries claw it back; no US treaty in many.

🚜 Ag equipment

Best: cheap secondhand in depopulating Japan / Italy / Spain; CAP grants help fund it.

Catch: import duties & thin dealer networks in frontier markets.

💶 Subsidies

Best: EU CAP young-farmer installation aid €50k+ (PT / IT / GR / ES).

Catch: attach to individual registered farmers, not the "commune."

Depopulating Europe subsidizes the exact things S5 said you'd need — if you can get the visa.

Before next session

Homework

  1. Run the questionnaire as your real group. Set the six sliders to what your actual members would weight, and write down your top three countries.
  2. Pin down the visa. For each of your top two, find the specific visa you'd each use — its income/asset threshold, how long it takes, and the path to permanent residency. (Start from each country's globe card.)
  3. Sketch the legal stack. One line each: who holds the land (trust / foundation / CLT) and who runs the farm (co-op / company) — and whether foreigners can even own land there.
  4. Find your dealbreaker. Name the single thing most likely to sink your top pick — a foreign-ownership ban? a closing visa? water / climate? — and write the one question you'd take to a local lawyer.
  5. Gut-check the trade. In two sentences: which corner of the triangle is your group giving up, and is everyone actually OK with it?
